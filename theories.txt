In academic writing, you often cite papers to **borrow their authority**, not because you are writing a book report on them. You only need to read enough to verify that they support the specific sentence you are writing.

Here is the **"Cheat Sheet"** so you can cite them confidently without drowning in reading.

### 1. The "Must-Skim" List (Read the Abstract & Intro only)

These two are the most critical because they directly explain *why* your method works.

* **DropEdge (Rong et al., ICLR 2020)**
* **Why read it:** This is your main defense against "accuracy loss." You are effectively doing a "smarter" version of this.
* **The Gist:** They prove that randomly removing edges during training acts as a regularizer (like Dropout), reducing overfitting and "over-smoothing" (where all nodes start looking the same).
* **Your Argument:** "Our stochastic materialization functions as a topology-aware variant of DropEdge [cite], enhancing generalization."


* **GraphSAGE (Hamilton et al., NeurIPS 2017)**
* **Why read it:** This is the grandfather of "neighbor sampling."
* **The Gist:** They showed you don't need the whole graph; you only need a fixed-size sample to learn good embeddings.
* **Your Argument:** "Consistent with the findings of Hamilton et al. [cite], we observe that a fixed-budget neighborhood sample provides an unbiased estimator of the aggregation function."



### 2. The "Just Cite It" List (Don't read, just trust the math)

These are heavy theoretical papers. You cite them to say, "The math exists."

* **FastGCN (Chen et al., ICLR 2018)**
* **Your Argument:** "Layer-wise independent sampling is a standard variance reduction technique in GNN training [cite]."


* **Li et al. (ICLR 2018) OR NT & Maehara (2019)**
* **Your Argument:** "Since GNNs fundamentally act as low-pass filters [cite], high-frequency noise introduced by sketching is naturally attenuated."



### 3. How to "Read" a Paper for Lit Review in 10 Minutes

If you *do* open them, follow this algorithm to save time:

1. **Read the Title & Abstract:** Does it sound relevant? (Yes).
2. **Ctrl+F "Conclusion":** Read the first paragraph of the conclusion. This usually restates the "Main Contribution" in plain English.
3. **Look at Figure 1:** Most deep learning papers summarize their whole idea in the first diagram.
4. **Close the PDF.**

**Summary:** You technically only need to understand the **concepts** (Regularization, Low-Pass Filtering, Unbiased Estimators). I have just explained those concepts to you. You can now cite the papers as the "origin" of these concepts without reading their proofs.
